What the research says

Four papers converge on the same finding: people don't fail to study because they lack motivation, they fail because good intentions don't automatically translate into action. Sheeran's 2002 meta-analysis showed that intentions explain only about a third of variance in actual behavior, the rest is the gap.

The most reliable fix is the implementation intention (Gollwitzer): a literal "if X, then I will Y" sentence that mentally pre-commits a specific response to a specific cue. Across 94 studies the effect was d = 0.65, bigger than most psychological interventions ever produce.

Three things make if-then plans work: (1) specificity, "study more" doesn't, "open chapter 5 at 9 AM at my desk" does; (2) real cues, time, place, or a specific feeling/urge, not abstract intent; (3) strong underlying goal, if you don't actually want to pass the exam, no formula helps.

Sirois & Pychyl (2013) add the kindness piece: procrastination is short-term mood repair, not laziness. Self-forgiveness for past missed sessions reduces future procrastination. So when a plan flops, log it, adjust the if-then, and move on.

Sources: Sheeran & Webb (2016) The Intention-Behavior Gap; Sheeran (2002) Intention-Behaviour Relations; Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) Implementation Intentions and Goal Attainment; Sirois & Pychyl (2013) Procrastination and Short-Term Mood Regulation.
