What the research says

Four papers converge on the same finding: people don't fail to study because they lack motivation, they fail because good intentions don't automatically translate into action. Sheeran's 2002 meta-analysis showed that intentions explain only about a third of variance in actual behavior, the rest is the gap.

The most reliable fix is the implementation intention (Gollwitzer): a literal "if X, then I will Y" sentence that mentally pre-commits a specific response to a specific cue. Across 94 studies the effect was d = 0.65, bigger than most psychological interventions ever produce.

Three things make if-then plans work: (1) specificity, "study more" doesn't, "open chapter 5 at 9 AM at my desk" does; (2) real cues, time, place, or a specific feeling/urge, not abstract intent; (3) strong underlying goal, if you don't actually want to pass the exam, no formula helps.

Sirois & Pychyl (2013) add the kindness piece: procrastination is short-term mood repair, not laziness. Self-forgiveness for past missed sessions reduces future procrastination. So when a plan flops, log it, adjust the if-then, and move on.

Cal Newport, Deep Work (2016) stacks neatly on top. Where the if-then plans answer "how do I follow through?", Deep Work answers "what should the blocks contain?" Three ideas from the book are baked into this app:

1. Schedule every minute. The empty hour is where intentions die. Newport gives every minute of the workday a job, in 30-min minimum blocks, in a notebook. The Calendar tab is the digital version. "Your goal is not to stick to a given schedule at all costs; it's instead to maintain, at all times, a thoughtful say in what you're doing with your time going forward." When a block breaks, you don't abandon the plan, you redraw it from now.

2. Deep vs shallow. Deep work pushes your limits and is hard to replace. Shallow work is logistical and easy. Newport's heuristic: ask how many months it would take to train a smart college grad to do the task. Many months = deep, a week = shallow. Aim for 50–70% deep; novices cap at ~1 hr/day of pure deep work, experts ~4 hrs.

3. Shutdown ritual. Unfinished tasks dominate attention until they're written down with a plan (the Zeigarnik effect, Baumeister & Masicampo). Newport's fix: a 5-step end-of-day routine ending in the literal phrase "Shutdown complete." Habit forms in 1–2 weeks. The Shutdown tab walks you through it.

Bonus: Newport's 4DX scoreboard idea — track lead measures (hours of deep work this week) instead of lag measures (your final grade). The History tab is your scoreboard.
