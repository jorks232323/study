What the research says

d = 0.65 →

Five sources converge on the same finding: people don't fail to study because they lack motivation, they fail because good intentions don't automatically translate into action. Sheeran's 2002 meta-analysis showed that intentions explain only about a third of variance in actual behavior. The rest is the gap.

The most reliable fix is the implementation intention (Gollwitzer): a literal "if X, then I will Y" sentence that mentally pre-commits a specific response to a specific cue. Across 94 studies the effect was d = 0.65, bigger than most psychological interventions ever produce.

Three things make if-then plans work: (1) specificity — "study more" doesn't, "open chapter 5 at 9 AM at my desk" does; (2) real cues — a time, place, or specific feeling; (3) strong underlying goal — if you don't actually want to pass the exam, no formula helps.

Sirois & Pychyl (2013) add the kindness piece: procrastination is short-term mood repair, not laziness. Self-forgiveness for past missed sessions reduces future procrastination. So when a plan flops, log it, adjust the if-then, and move on.

Cal Newport's Deep Work stacks neatly on top. Where the if-then plans answer "how do I follow through?", Deep Work answers "what should the blocks contain?" Three ideas from the book are baked into this app:

1. Schedule every minute. The empty hour is where intentions die. Newport gives every minute of the workday a job, in 30-min minimum blocks. The Calendar tab is the digital version. When a block breaks, you don't abandon the plan — you redraw it from now.

2. Deep vs shallow. Deep work pushes your limits and is hard to replace. Shallow work is logistical and easy. Newport's heuristic: ask how many months it would take to train a smart college grad to do the task. Many = deep, a week = shallow. Aim for 50–70% deep; novices cap at ~1 hr/day, experts ~4 hrs.

3. Shutdown ritual. Unfinished tasks dominate attention until they're written down with a plan (the Zeigarnik effect, Baumeister & Masicampo). Newport's fix: a 5-step end-of-day routine ending in the literal phrase "Shutdown complete." Habit forms in 1–2 weeks.

Bonus: Newport's 4DX scoreboard idea — track lead measures (deep work hours this week) instead of lag measures (final grade). The History tab is your scoreboard.
